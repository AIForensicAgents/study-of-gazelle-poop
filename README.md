# study-of-gazelle-poop
A playful but professional field guide and dataset for the study of gazelle scat (droppings, feces) — designed for educators, field biologists, and curious naturalists.

---

## Overview
This repository collects observations, notes, and imagery documenting gazelle scat morphology, context, and clues about diet and habitat. The goal is to make field-identification and non-invasive ecological inference accessible, repeatable, and scientifically sound — with a light touch of humor because biology can be fun.

What you'll find here:
- Field notes and standardized observation templates
- A curated image gallery for training and educational use
- Guidance on interpreting scat features in ecological context
- Ethics and safety guidance for responsibly working in the field

---

## Why it matters
Gazelle feces are more than an awkward roadside sight — they are compact ecological data packs. Scat studies provide:
- Non-invasive insight into diet, health, and parasite loads
- Indicators of population distribution and habitat use
- Baseline data for conservation management and restoration planning
- Material for educational modules teaching field methods and natural history

Well-documented scat datasets enable reproducible science and support conservation decisions without needing to handle animals.

## Morphology notes
Use a consistent vocabulary when recording scat features. Below are field-applicable traits to record with examples and interpretation hints.

- **Form**
  - Pelleted vs clumped mass: many gazelles produce small, firm pellets; clumped masses may indicate species-specific consolidation or age/condition effects.
- **Size**
  - Pellets: length ~5–20 mm, diameter ~3–8 mm (species/age dependent). Use a ruler or scale bar in photos.
- **Shape**
  - Cylindrical, spherical, or irregular clumps. Rounded pellets that are consistent in size often indicate healthy adult grazers.
- **Color**
  - Fresh dark brown to black; older droppings fade to grayish or bleached by sun.
- **Texture**
  - Firm and dry vs soft/moist: moisture and consistency provide clues to recent diet and hydration.
- **Content visible to naked eye**
  - Plant fibers, grass cuticle, seeds, twigs. Note notable inclusions (e.g., thorn fragments).
- **Olfactory signs**
  - Strong musky or grassy smell in fresh samples — record only qualitatively and with caution.
- **Age indicators**
  - Moisture, sheen, insect activity, and color fading help estimate freshness (hours to weeks).

Record each trait with a timestamp, GPS coordinates, and photo at standard distances (e.g., close-up with scale + contextual landscape shot).

## Habitat / diet clues
Interpret scat features alongside habitat context for better ecological inferences.

- **Grazing vs browsing**
  - High proportion of grass fragments and fine cuticle → grazing. Presence of woody fragments, leaves, or shrub bark → browsing.
- **Seasonality**
  - Seed fragments or fruit pulp may indicate seasonal feeding events. Drier seasons can produce more compact, fibre-rich pellets.
- **Water and health**
  - Very loose, watery feces may indicate gastrointestinal upset, parasite burden, or recent high-water/green forage intake.
- **Habitat markers**
  - Scat found on game trails, near salt licks, or below favored feeding patches gives mobility and foraging behavior clues.
- **Co-occurrence**
  - Note tracks, bite marks on plants, and proximity to other species' sign to infer interactions or competition.

Whenever possible, pair scat observations with habitat photos and plant ID notes.

## Ethics & safety
Fieldwork must prioritize safety, legal compliance, and animal welfare. Key points:

- **Non-invasive only**
  - Do not trap, handle, or otherwise disturb animals for the sake of scat collection.
- **Permits & land access**
  - Obtain required permits and landowner or protected-area permissions before conducting surveys.
- **Biosafety**
  - Wear disposable gloves and use tools (tongs, scoops) when handling scat. Avoid direct contact.
  - Use sealed containers and label samples clearly. Clean and disinfect equipment between sites.
- **Human health**
  - Gazelle scat can carry zoonotic pathogens and parasites. Do not eat, taste, or intentionally aerosolize samples.
- **Cultural & legal sensitivity**
  - Respect local regulations and cultural norms about fauna and fieldwork.
- **Data ethics**
  - When publishing location-sensitive data (e.g., on endangered populations), consider obfuscation or access controls to avoid facilitating poaching or harm.

Include a short safety checklist in field packs and training for all team members.

## Future work
Opportunities to expand the project and make the dataset more useful:

- Standardized field forms and mobile data-collection app (with offline GPS and photo sync)
- DNA metabarcoding to link scat content to plant species and gut microbiome studies
- Parasite screening and health indicators (paired with veterinary oversight)
- Machine learning model for automated scat classification from images
- GIS mapping and habitat-use modelling based on scat encounter rates
- Educational modules and citizen-science workflows with clear QA/QC
- Expand to comparative scat datasets across gazelle species and sympatric ungulates
